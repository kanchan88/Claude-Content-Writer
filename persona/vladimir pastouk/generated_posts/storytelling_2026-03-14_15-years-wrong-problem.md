# Topic: I practiced dentistry for 15 years before realizing I was solving the wrong problem

---

For 15 years, every patient sat in my chair for the same reason.

And for 15 years, I gave them the wrong answer.

Not because I was careless. Because I was trained.

Cavity? Fill it.
Infection? Treat it.
Gum disease? Manage it.

That's what dental school taught. And I was good at it.

But the patients kept coming back.

The infections returned. The inflammation stayed. The fatigue never lifted.

And slowly, I started seeing something no textbook had prepared me for.

A baby who couldn't breastfeed. A restriction under the tongue nobody had checked.

An executive on CPAP, still waking up exhausted. An airway obstruction no sleep doctor had traced to the mouth.

A woman with chronic headaches for years. Jaw tension, tongue restriction, breathing pattern nobody had connected.

Different patients. Different symptoms. Same place nobody was looking.

The mouth.

Not just the teeth. The airway behind them. The tongue. The tissue. The inflammation quietly traveling through the bloodstream to the brain, the heart, the gut.

I wasn't solving the wrong problems because I was a bad dentist.

I was solving them because I was a good one. Doing exactly what I was trained to do.

And what I was trained to do was incomplete.

That realization cost me 15 years.

But it gave me the next 15.

I built a patented surgical technique for the restrictions I kept finding. I founded Glossodontics to teach other dentists what dental school never covered. And I co-founded Aligness with Dr. Heather Skeens, a surgeon who saw the same gaps from the other side of medicine.

She went upstream. I went outward.

Same conclusion.

The mouth isn't a separate organ. It's the front door to the entire body. And almost nobody is watching it.

If your health team has checked everything above the neck and below the chest but never looked at what connects them, the answer might be sitting in the last place anyone thought to check.

Sometimes the confession is the credential.

## Self Comments

### 1. Personal Angle
The hardest part wasn't the clinical discovery.

It was sitting across from a patient who'd seen six specialists, spent thousands, and still had no answers. Knowing the answer was right there. In a place nobody had thought to look.

That kind of quiet frustration is what eventually pushes you to build something new.

### 2. Trend / Thought
Dental schools are starting to introduce airway screening into their curriculum. Slowly.

But we're still a decade away from it being standard practice. Which means right now, the dentists who see these patterns are mostly teaching themselves.

That gap between what the research says and what the profession teaches is where the biggest clinical breakthroughs are hiding.

### 3. Science / Data
People with periodontal disease carry 2-3x the cardiovascular risk, according to research reviewed by the American Heart Association.

The same oral bacteria linked to gum disease, Porphyromonas gingivalis, has been found in brain tissue of Alzheimer's patients.

The mouth isn't just connected to the body. It's driving outcomes nobody is screening for.

### 4. Post Value Amplification
The line most people will scroll past but shouldn't:

"I wasn't solving the wrong problems because I was a bad dentist. I was solving them because I was a good one."

That's the trap of expertise. You get so good at the trained answer that you stop questioning whether the question was right. It applies way beyond dentistry.
