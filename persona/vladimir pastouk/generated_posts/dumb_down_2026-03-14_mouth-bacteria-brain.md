# Topic: How mouth bacteria travels to the brain - 60-second explanation

---

Your mouth is quietly sending bacteria to your brain.

No specialist on your health team is checking for it.

Here's the 60-second version:

Your mouth is full of bacteria. That's normal.

But when your gums are inflamed or infected, those bacteria slip into your bloodstream.

Every time you chew. Every time you brush over bleeding gums.

Once in the blood, they travel.

Some of them reach your brain.

And when they get there, they don't just pass through.

They trigger inflammation in brain tissue.

That inflammation starts breaking down brain cells. Slowly. Quietly. Over years.

Here's the simple chain:

Infected gums → bacteria enters blood → travels to the brain → triggers inflammation → brain cells break down over time

The same bacteria behind gum disease has been found in brain tissue of Alzheimer's patients.

(Dominy et al., Science Advances, 2019)

Now here's the part nobody talks about:

Your cardiologist didn't check for this.
Your neurologist didn't check for this.
Your functional medicine doctor didn't check for this.

Because none of them look inside your mouth.

The mouth is not separate from the body.

It's the front door.

And for most people, nobody is watching it.

### 2. Trend / Thought
The research connecting oral bacteria to brain inflammation has accelerated in the last three years.

We're heading toward a future where your dentist visit isn't just about your teeth. It's a neurological screening you didn't know you needed.

The mouth is finally being recognized as the diagnostic gateway it always was.

### 3. Science / Data
People with periodontal disease have 2-3x the risk of heart attack and stroke, according to research reviewed by the American Heart Association.

The same inflammatory pathways that link the mouth to the heart also reach the brain.

One entry point. Multiple downstream consequences.

### 4. Post Value Amplification
The part most people will skip over but shouldn't:

"Every time you brush over bleeding gums."

Bleeding gums are not normal. They're an open door between your mouth and your bloodstream. Most people treat it as minor. It's not. It's the moment bacteria gets access to the rest of your body.
