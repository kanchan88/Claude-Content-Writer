# Topic: I left a career most doctors would never leave — here's why

---

People called me crazy when I left a lucrative surgical career.

They were right.

The income was high. The respect was huge. The identity was everything.

For 25 years, I was a board-certified corneal surgeon. Fellowship-trained. Published. Internationally invited to speak. I performed some of the most technically precise procedures in all of medicine — transplanting tissue measured in microns.

And the outcomes were good.

Until I started noticing what kept bringing patients back.

The cornea I transplanted would fail. Not because the surgery was wrong. Because the body underneath was inflamed — and no one was asking why.

I'd look through my slit lamp and see something no other specialist was looking for:

→ Systemic inflammation showing up in the eye before it appeared on a single blood panel.
→ Autoimmune patterns that three other doctors had missed — because none of them were looking through this lens.
→ Metabolic dysfunction driving tissue failure that no surgical technique could outrun.

I wasn't treating eye disease anymore. I was watching the entire body fall apart — one cornea at a time.

And the question that changed everything wouldn't stop:

Why did the tissue fail in the first place?

That question pulled me upstream. Out of the OR. Into functional medicine training. Into systems thinking. Into looking at the whole patient instead of one organ.

Then my brother-in-law was diagnosed with cancer.

And I watched — from inside the family, not inside a clinic — exactly what coordination failure looks like when the stakes are someone you love.

His oncologist never spoke to his surgeon. His surgeon never spoke to his internist. Every specialist was excellent. The system connecting them was nonexistent.

I had spent 25 years inside that same system. Operating brilliantly inside a structure that was architecturally broken.

So I wrote Unshackled — publicly naming what medicine does to doctors and patients when coordination doesn't exist.

And then I co-founded Aligness.

Not a clinic. Not a wellness brand. A strategic health advisory — the coordination architecture that medicine never built.

35% of physicians say they want to leave medicine. Most never will. Because there's no alternative model to leave for.

I didn't leave because I was burned out.

I left because 25 years in the OR showed me something I couldn't unsee.

And then I built the model that should have existed all along.

## Self Comments

### Comment 1 (Personal)

The hardest part wasn't leaving. It was the silence after. When you leave surgery, colleagues don't argue. They just stop calling. The identity of "surgeon" is so deeply wired that stepping away reads as failure — even when the evidence is what moved you. That silence taught me more about medicine's culture than 25 years inside it.

### Comment 2 (Trend)

The physician exodus isn't about burnout. It's about architecture. Doctors are trained to be specialists — not system designers. The ones who see the coordination failure most clearly have the fewest tools to fix it. That's the real crisis no one is naming.

### Comment 3 (Value Add)

Every surgeon has had the upstream question. That moment you realize you're treating consequences, not causes. Most push it aside because the system doesn't reward asking it. The ones who can't push it aside either burn out or build something new. There's no middle ground.
