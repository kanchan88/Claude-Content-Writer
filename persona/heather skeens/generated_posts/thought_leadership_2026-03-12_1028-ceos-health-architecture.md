# Topic: 1,028 CEOs left in 5 months — the problem isn't workload, it's health architecture

---

1,028 CEOs left their roles in the first 5 months of 2025.

The highest January-May total ever recorded (Challenger, Gray & Christmas).

71% of CEOs report burnout (Vistage, 2025).

Everyone is talking about workload. Almost nobody is asking about health architecture.

Deloitte's 2025 research found something that should change this conversation entirely:

Cognitive strain and decision friction now surpass workload volume as the leading indicators of executive burnout.

Not hours worked. Not email volume. Not board pressure.

The brain itself is underperforming.

And here's what no one connects:

These executives have 5–8 specialists. Endocrinologist. Cardiologist. Functional medicine doctor. Sleep specialist.

→ None of them communicate with each other.
→ None of them have a unified picture of that executive's health.
→ The executive is their own chief medical officer — while running a company.

You wouldn't run a Fortune 500 where your CFO, CTO, and COO never talk.

Why do you run your health that way?

This isn't a burnout problem.

It's a coordination failure with a cognitive price tag.

The most expensive decision a CEO makes isn't on the balance sheet.

It's the one they make at 2pm with a brain running at 70%.

## Self Comments

### Comment 1 (Personal)

25 years in the OR showed me the pattern clearly. The executives who came in with the worst outcomes weren't the ones who ignored their health. They were the ones with five excellent specialists and zero coordination between them. The architecture was missing, not the effort.

### Comment 2 (Trend)

The Challenger data tells a bigger story than resignation. When cognitive strain overtakes workload as the primary burnout driver, we're no longer looking at a scheduling problem. We're looking at a biological performance problem that no executive coach or time management system can solve.

### Comment 3 (Health)

Brain fog at 2pm isn't a coffee problem. Systemic inflammation, undiagnosed airway restriction, and fragmented lab interpretation compound silently. By the time a CEO notices decision fatigue, the upstream drivers have been running unchecked for years. Nobody connected the dots because nobody was looking at the full picture.

### Comment 4 (Value Add)

Here's a question every executive should ask their health team: "When was the last time all of my specialists were in the same conversation about me?" If the answer is never — you don't have a health strategy. You have a collection of appointments.
