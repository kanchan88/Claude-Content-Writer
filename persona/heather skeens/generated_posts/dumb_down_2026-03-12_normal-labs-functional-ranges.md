# Topic: Normal labs vs functional ranges — why "in range" doesn't mean optimal

---

Your blood sugar is "normal."

But you can't think clearly after lunch.

Your thyroid is "in range."

But you're exhausted by 2pm every single day.

Your cholesterol is "fine."

But your cardiologist has never seen your inflammatory markers trending upward for 3 years straight.

Here's what your doctor means when they say "normal":

→ You are not clinically sick enough to treat.

Here's what they don't mean:

→ You are performing optimally.

Standard lab ranges are population averages. They tell you where you fall compared to everyone — including the unhealthy.

"Normal" fasting glucose: 70–99 mg/dL.
Functional optimal range: 75–86 mg/dL.

You can be "normal" at 98 and already insulin resistant.

"Normal" TSH: 0.5–4.5 mIU/L.
Functional optimal range: 1.0–2.0 mIU/L.

You can be "normal" at 4.0 and barely functioning.

1 in 4 doctor visits involves medically unexplained symptoms.

Not because the symptoms aren't real.

Because the ranges being used weren't designed to detect performance decline. They were designed to detect disease.

And here's what almost no one tells executives:

A single lab snapshot is a photograph. What you actually need is a trend line.

One "normal" result means nothing without knowing the direction it's moving.

Your labs don't need a second opinion.

They need an architect who reads the trajectory — not just the number.

## Self Comments

### Comment 1 (Personal)

After 25 years in medicine, the phrase I've heard most from executives is: "My doctor says everything is fine, but I don't feel fine." That gap between the lab result and the lived experience is where the real clinical work begins. And almost nobody is doing it.

### Comment 2 (Trend)

The shift from snapshot diagnostics to longitudinal trend analysis is the single biggest unlock in executive health.

A fasting glucose of 92 means nothing in isolation.

But 92 trending from 78 over three years tells a completely different story. Direction matters more than position.

---

Ask your doctor this one question at your next visit:
"Can you show me the trend of this marker over the last 3 years?"

If the answer is "we don't track that" — you don't have a health strategy.
You have a series of disconnected photographs.
